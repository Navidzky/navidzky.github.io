About

I am a Ph.D. Candidate in Economics at Purdue University and will be on the job market in 2026–2027. My job market paper studies efficiency in decentralized transportation markets, examining how geography shapes competition, matching, and welfare when travelers and carriers interact strategically across locations.

My research lies at the intersection of macroeconomics, search theory, and transport economics.

Research

Job Market Paper Working Paper
Decentralized Transportation Markets: A Global Directed Search Approach
Navid Safahieh
I study how geography shapes competition, matching, and welfare in decentralized transportation markets. I develop a dynamic directed-search model in which travelers and carriers can match remotely before the carrier arrives. Travelers and carriers strategically choose where to apply and what requests to accept, and unmatched carriers relocate across locations. Location affects not only costs but also market access: attractive locations have thicker demand but stronger competition, while peripheral carriers may reposition to improve future prospects. I characterize equilibrium prices, matching probabilities, and relocation flows, and compare them to the socially efficient allocation. The model highlights how spatial frictions and remote matching shape matching patterns and the geographic allocation of capacity, with implications for ride-hailing and dry bulk shipping markets.
Working Paper
When Outside Options Shape the Labor Market: A Directed Search Perspective
Navid Safahieh
This paper studies how differences in outside options across workers affect wages, firm entry, and job allocation. I develop a directed search model with skilled and unskilled workers and high-tech and low-tech firms. Results show that stronger outside options raise wages for skilled workers but can reduce high-tech firm entry, potentially lowering overall wage opportunities.

The Heterogeneous Effects of Exogenous Shocks on Public Transportation Demand
With Kowsar Yousefi and Amir Samimi
A sudden gas price shock increased subway usage by approximately 3.9% (cross-price elasticity ≈ 0.044), while the COVID-19 pandemic reduced subway ridership by approximately 42.6%.

Modeling the Accuracy of Traffic Crash Prediction Models
With Mohammad Hesam Rashidi, Soheil Keshavarz, Parham Pazari, and Amir Samimi
Modeling the forecasting accuracy of traffic crash prediction across 31 provinces of Iran. Key predictors include crash variability, historical crash counts, employment ratio, GDP per capita, and traffic exposure.
